Pan-Islamic Socialist Nationalism

القومية الاشتراكية الإسلامية

A synthesis of al-Afghani's civilizational solidarity, Iqbal's metaphysics of collective selfhood, and the Islamic socialist tradition — forged in the crucible of colonial modernity.

I · The Simple Motto

"The Ummah is the Nation;
the Earth is its Commons;
Justice is its Sovereign."

After al-Afghani · Iqbal · al-Siba'i · Q. 21:105 · Q. 2:143

II · The Complex Motto
"

Where al-Afghani's al-'Urwah al-Wuthqa (العروة الوثقى) — the indissoluble bond — is reforged not merely as creed but as the 'Asabiyyah of the colonized and the dispossessed; where Iqbal's Khudi (خودی), the inviolable self that will not bend before throne or capital, is scaled from the individual soul to the collective will of a people; and where Zakat ceases to be episodic almsgiving and becomes the permanent, non-negotiable architecture of the common good — only there does the Ummah become what history has always demanded but never yet delivered: a sovereignty answering to no empire, no market, and no earthly throne, but to the divine covenant between God and the faithful poor.

After Ibn Khaldun · al-Afghani · Iqbal · Shariati · Q. 2:275–279 · Q. 21:105 · Q. 49:13

Intellectual Genealogy

I · Pan-Islamism

Jamāl al-Dīn al-Afghānī (1838–1897)

"al-'Urwah al-Wuthqa" (1884). The Ummah recast as civilizational bloc against Western hegemony and the colonial logic of divide-and-rule.

II · Philosophy of Selfhood

Muḥammad Iqbāl (1877–1938)

"Asrār-e-Khudī" (1915); "Reconstruction of Religious Thought" (1930). The Ummah as spiritual nation; Khudi as inviolable collective dignity.

III · Historical Sociology

Ibn Khaldūn (1332–1406)

"Muqaddimah" (1377). 'Asabiyyah (group solidarity) as the engine of civilizations — reinterpreted here as solidarity built on shared dispossession, not blood.

IV · Islamic Socialism

Muṣṭafā al-Sibā'ī (1915–1964)

"Ishtirākiyyat al-Islām" (1959). Islam inherently socialist through Zakat, prohibition of Ribā, and the theology of Istikhlāf (divine stewardship of the earth).

V · Revolutionary Theology

ʿAlī Sharīʿatī (1933–1977)

Fanonian Third Worldism fused with Shi'a theology. Islam as ideology of the Mustaz'afin (Q. 4:75). The language of Islamic liberation theology.

VI · Revelatory Sources

The Quran · Foundational Texts

Q. 2:143 (Ummatan Wasaṭan); Q. 2:275–279 (ban on Ribā/usury); Q. 21:105 (the righteous inherit the earth); Q. 49:13 (unity across human difference).
